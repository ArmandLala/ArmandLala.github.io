PAPER ARCHITECTURE

The "PAPER ARCHITECTURE" project examines the development of complex surfaces in architecture (and beyond). Complex forms represent a significant challenge, from their conceptualization to physical realization. Ideally, a complex surface can be assimilated to any known object, with either a regular or irregular shape.

Building on these concepts, our project involves "tessellating" these surfaces with patterns of varying complexity to create the components needed for their final construction. In geometry, the simplest element to manage is the point, which allows for infinite combinations. We envisioned controlling double-curved complex surfaces through the use of geometric points. Starting from primary splines (connections between points with the same number of elements), we were able to control the primitive shape of the surface and deform it at will. This strategy enables the creation of infinite configurations by varying the position of the primary points on the two main projection planes of space, according to the method of Monge in Descriptive Geometry. A "backbone" spline regulates the general shape's trajectory, akin to the structure of an annelid or a snake.

Parametric management is crucial for creating and controlling the constituent pieces of the structure. Using the "Sequence" command in GeoGebra, we developed an algorithm to divide the "backbone" spline into n points, from which perpendicular lines are generated that regulate the division in one of the two main directions (V). With the same algorithm, leveraging the lines created by the primary spline points and the perpendicular lines of the backbone, we created the parametric subdivision in the U direction. The result is an "adaptive grid" entirely managed through points and splines/segments, visualized via the Monge method.

For the module adaptation, the most effective solution was to generate an offset of the complex surface and develop a system to manage the opening of each truncated pyramid module and the overall offset distance. Constructing the surface and making it manipulable by the user posed several challenges. Studying these surfaces and their management in software like Rhino inspired us to use the primary splines to control their curvature and implement parametric subdivision, similar to what is used in Grasshopper. In Rhino, surfaces are generated more automatically, while in GeoGebra, although more cumbersome, the points-and-splines system offers an effective hierarchical management that integrates seamlessly with the constraints of descriptive geometry (parallelisms, intersections, projections, visualizations, dimensions, etc.).

Using points as the basic element simplified the process in almost every aspect within GeoGebra Geometry. By identifying each point and its position in space, we were able to group the points of each module and, through a flipping in projective space, achieve flattening, i.e., the representation of the actual dimensions of each module’s faces. By recognizing the shared edges between the modules, we developed several configurations for the final pieces (pieces along the U or V direction, single modules, or a continuous single module, though with some limitations). The assembly is carried out through dovetail joints.

This approach allows for a clean, parametric, and controllable management of the final complex surface. The main goal is to reduce material waste and costs in the realization of complex architecture, especially using alternative materials to conventional ones. In addition to optimizing costs and reducing waste, our method aims to increase accessibility to this type of construction, which is typically too expensive and slow.

At an urban scale, the "PAPER ARCHITECTURE" method adapts to urban voids, creating unique structures such as organic green roofs, pavilions, or urban furniture. In summary, the project combines efficiency and sustainability with accessibility and flexibility, optimizing the various scales of architecture.
